Introduction

  • Common Pool Resources (CPR) are goods that are rivalrous and non-exclusive (fisheries, groundwater, forest etc)
  • subjects to the tragedy of the commons (Hardin 1968)
  • CPRs mainly studied in a static framework (one-shot or repeated game)
  • dynamic framework could be more realistic, to capture the evolution of the resource over time
  • dynamic framework can be either in discrete-time or in continuous-time
  • vast majority of the CPR literature that combines theory and experimentation in the lab is in discrete time (e.g. Herr et al. 1997, Gardner et al. 1997, Hey et al. 2009, Suter et al. 2012)
  • dynamic models in continuous-time over an infinite horizon are widely used to model economic problems (Dockner et al. 2000)
  • very few of these models are tested in the laboratory, except Tasneem et al. (2017, 2019)

We consider a simple linear quadratic model in which agents exploit a renewable resource over an infinite horizon (Rubio & Casino 2003)

Research questions
  • Do the experimental subjects behave as predicted by the model ?
  • What is the impact of the introduction of interactions in this dynamic context?
What we do
  • we define theoretical benchmarks
  • we define an experimental protocol to test the model in the lab
  • we compare the observations to the theoretical behaviors, in a single agent situation (optimal control problem) and in a two-players game (differential game)

Model

  • simple linear quadratic model in which two agents \(i\) and \(j\) exploit a renewable resource \((H)\)
  • extraction from the resource generates a revenue \(B(w) = aw -\frac{b}{2}w^2\)
  • and a cost \(C(H, w) = max(0, c_0-c_1H)w\)
    • the cost depends negatively on the level of resource (H)
    • the cost is positive when the resource level is lower than \(\frac{c_0}{c_1}\) and null if equal or higher
      piecewise function to avoid subsidy in case of a high level of resource
  • players' instantaneous payoff is given by the difference between revenue and cost

Total discounted payoff, with \(r\) the discount rate, is given by

\(\int_{0}^{\infty}e^{-rt} \left[ aw_i(t)-\frac{b}{2}w_i(t)^{2}-\max (0,\; c_{0}-c_{1}H(t))w_i(t) \right]dt\)

with

\(\dot H(t)=R-\alpha(w_i(t)+w_j(t))\)

\(R\) is the natural recharge and \(\alpha\) is the return flow coefficient

we distinguish 3 benchmarks

  • social optimum: both players in the pair maximize the joint discounted net payoffs and maintain the resource at an efficient level (cooperative solution)
  • feedback: players maximize their own discounted net payoffs, they adopt a non-cooperative strategy
  • myopic: at each instant players maximize their current payoff regardless of the evolution of the resource

Choice of parameters such that theoretical paths are disctincts

Experimental design

Characteristics of continuous time
  • players can change their extraction rate whenever they want
  • extraction rate and resource evolve continuously
Implementation of continuous time in the lab
  • time in the experiment is synchronized with real time
  • extraction rate transmitted by the player's computer to the server every second
  • calculations made every seconds and information updated every seconds on the players' screens
Infinite horizon
  • through the player's payoff
  • each instant \(p\) we add to the discounted cumulative payoff (from \(t=0\) to \(t=p\)) the discounted payoff from the current instant to infinity (from \(t=p\) to \(t=\infty\)) assuming that the extraction rate remains unchanged
  • the subject can observe the consequences of this extraction rate over the very long run
  • subjects play the same amount of time
  • subjects play alone first (optimal control problem)
    two 5 minutes trials followed by a 5 minutes play for real
  • same subjects (within subject design) then play by groups of 2 (differential game)
    two 5 minutes trials followed by a 5 minutes play for real
    random rematching after each round
  • 70 participants, students from the University of Montpellier
  • 70 observations for the optimal control, and 35 for the two-players game

Results

  • over-exploitation at the beginning (higher in the game)
  • more dispersion in the game

Profiles

  • determine wether individuals and groups exhibited myopic, feeback or optimal behavior
  • based on the Mean Squared Deviation between the observed path and the theoretical one
  • if the player/group 's \(MSD_{MY}\) is lower than his \(MSD_{OS}\) (and \(MSD_{FEED}\) in the game) the player/group is classified as Myopic
  • we considered the conditional MSD (with respect to the \(t - 1\) resource level) and applied regressions to check that the coefficient of the conditional decisions corresponding to the lowest MSD was actually different from zéro
  • we therefore classified players / groups as "significantly" optimal, myopic, feedback or undetermined (if the coefficient is not significantly different from zero)
Optimal control problem
  • 27.14% optimal and 72.86% undetermined
  • visual inspection of the undetermined and definition of new profiles: convergent (21.43%) and under-exploiter (24.29%)
  • remains 27.14% without any particular pattern
Game
  • 20% optimal and 80% undetermined
  • visual inspection of the undetermined : 25.71% of convergent and 14.29% of under-exploiter
  • a new profile: over-exploiter (17.14%) - between feedback and myopic
  • remains 22.86% without any particular pattern

Conclusion

  • provide a continuous-time model over an infinite horizon and an experimental protocol to test its predicition in the lab
  • we use a within-subject design to test the effect of interactions in this setting
  • nearly 20-25% of individuals and groups succeed in playing significantly optimal
  • another 20-25% play optimally but over a finite horizon, the category we called convergent
  • we observe no effect of the nature of time in the control but strong effects in the game
  • as expected strategic interaction increases the tragedy of the common
